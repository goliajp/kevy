## Honest framing of the strict-gate verdict

User's standard was `"可以学习思路，不可能更慢"`. This polish round
proved:

- **Where the lever existed and was clean**: pulled, won. kevy-uring
  cross-lang bench now shows kevy at kernel floor; kevy-resp now
  shows it beating the C reference, not just the Rust one.
- **Where the lever didn't help measurably**: reverted. kevy-map
  prefetch-during-probe regressed on cache-resident workloads
  (M4-Pro L2 = 16 MB, every benchmark size fits; prefetch helps
  only when probes spill out of L2, which our 7/8-load good-hash
  test cases don't).
- **Where the lever required giving up a charter property**: not
  pulled. kevy-hash AES-NI route would break
  `#![forbid(unsafe_code)]`; bare-Fx (no fmix64) would break the
  `no_catastrophic_clustering_on_low_entropy_keys` test.

Neither remaining gap (kevy-map insert, kevy-hash 1 ns noise) is
closeable through "learning from open-source competitors" because
the open-source winners win by sacrificing properties we keep. They
are properties of our design choice, not gaps in our implementation.

## Round 2 addendum — branchless set_meta closes the gap (P20)

Re-opened the kevy-map insert question (A1 had concluded "no clean
lever, gap structural"). Read hashbrown 0.15 `src/raw/mod.rs` line
2477 directly and found the actual lever:

> Our `set_meta(i, v)` had an `if i < GROUP_WIDTH - 1` branch around
> the mirror-byte write. Hashbrown uses the closed-form formula
> `index2 = ((i - GW) & mask) + GW` that always lands inside a
> `cap + GW`-sized buffer — when `i < GW` it's the mirror byte
> (`cap + i`), when `i >= GW` it equals `i` itself (no-op duplicate
> write). **Branchless.** Required extending our metadata buffer by
> 1 byte (`cap + GW - 1` → `cap + GW`).

Bench (5-run min-of-medians, M4-Pro, byte-string keys):

| workload | Round 1 (v0.1.1-rc) | Round 2 (post-P20) | hashbrown | post-P20 gap |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| insert_n256 | 15 | **13** | 13 | ✅ **TIE** |
| insert_n4096 | 17 | **12** | 11 | +1 ns (noise floor) |
| insert_n65536 | 22 | **16** | 14 | +1-2 ns (noise floor) |
| get_hit_n256 | 4 | 3-4 | 3 | 0-1 ns (often TIE) |
| get_hit_n4096 | 4 | 4 | 4 | ✅ TIE |
| get_hit_n65536 | 5 | 6 | 5 | +1 ns (slight slip, noise floor) |

**Insert gap was 2-8 ns → now 0-2 ns of noise floor.** The "structural
floor" verdict from A1 was wrong. The right lever existed; it just
required reading the open-source reference more carefully than
attempting prefetch (which was the obvious-looking lever and the
wrong one).

All 38 kevy-map tests + miri pass under the new layout.

### Methodological lesson

A1's "structural floor" verdict was a **process failure**: chose the
visible-looking lever (prefetch) and concluded "no lever exists" after
it didn't help. The right move was to read the reference code line-
by-line and find what we did differently. Round 2 took ~20 minutes of
hashbrown source-reading to find the actual lever.

**Future polish rounds: when an open-source reference is faster,
ALWAYS read its source for the hot path before declaring a structural
floor.** Reverting to "this is as good as it gets" without the
source-read step risks repeating A1's mistake.
